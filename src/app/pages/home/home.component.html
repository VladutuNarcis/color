Artistic definition
Color is an element of art and design, characterized by hue, value (lightness or darkness), and intensity (saturation or purity). It is used to evoke emotions, create contrast, and convey meaning in visual compositions.

Understanding the Physical phenomena

Color is how we see different wavelengths of light. Light is made up of tiny waves, and each color has a different wavelength. For example, red light has longer waves, while blue light has shorter ones. When light hits an object, some wavelengths are absorbed, and others are reflected.

When light enters our eyes, it passes through the lens and focuses on the retina at the back of the eye. The retina contains special cells called cones and rods. Cones detect color and are sensitive to three types of light: red, green, and blue. These primary colors combine to let us see the full spectrum. Rods are more sensitive to light and help us see in dim conditions, but they don't detect color. The cones send signals through the optic nerve to the brain's visual cortex. Here, the brain processes the information from the cones, combining the signals to determine the specific color we see.

History of colors

Can you imagine a world without colour? Today, we have yellow, pastel yellow, shiny yellow, yellow ochre—and the list goes on. Recognizing colours and giving them some meaning has become a way of life, but how did we begin with creating a palette of these beautiful colours? A historical look at colour offers a more cultural perspective, defined by time and place rather than association.
Throughout history, humans have sourced pigments from natural materials to create dyes and paints.
Tyrian purple, a dye favored by Phoenician and Roman emperors, was produced by extracting mucus from Murex sea snails.
Ultramarine, a vibrant blue pigment, was derived from a mineral called lapis lazuli, mined in Afghanistan.
In China, imperial yellow was obtained from the golden foxglove plant and was reserved exclusively for the emperor and his officials.
“Dead man’s head” was one part oil, one part amber resin, and too ​­many ​­parts Homo sapiens. It got its brown tint from the flesh, bones, and bandages of well-preserved Egyptian corpses. Fittingly, artists used it for skin tones..
Chemist William Perkin accidentally invented his eponymous purple while trying to synthesize the malaria treatment quinine from coal tar in 1856. Victorians adored it, but what we call “mauve” today is a more demure shade.
COLORS OVER THE WORLD
Colours convey an idea or a message in a powerful way. Across the globe, the way different cultures see colours varies drastically

Yellow

Eastern And Asian Cultures
Yellow is often worn by the members of the royal ruling class as it is believed to be sacred and imperial. In Japan, it is worn by rulers as it associated with bravery and courage. In India, it is the colour of commerce.
Western Cultures (North America And Europe)
It is associated with warmth, summer and hospitality in most Western nations. In the United States, it is associated with transportation where one can see school buses, taxis and street signage in yellow. In Germany, it is associated with envy.
Middle East
In the Middle East, it is widely connected to happiness and prosperity, just like in the Western cultures.

Pink

Eastern And Asian Cultures
It is considered feminine in the East and signifies marriage. In Korea, it is associated with trust. For several years, the Chinese did not recognize the colour but have welcomed it now in their culture due to Western influence.
Western Cultures (North American And Europe)
Pink is the colour of femininity and indicates the birth of a daughter. It also represents fun, childhood and sweetness.
Middle East
It does not hold any distinct meaning in Middle Eastern cultures.
